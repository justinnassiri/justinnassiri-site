Here is a pattern I have watched play out more than a hundred times. A CEO is active on LinkedIn about four times a year. They announce when the company raises money. They are "humbled" to be featured in a major article. They post when they need to hire a key employee. They reshare something from the company page.

Active quarterly. All of it about the company. And then they wonder why LinkedIn does nothing for them.

## The shift that works

We move these leaders from four times a year to **three to five times a week** — and we change what they post. Instead of company announcements, the mix looks like this:

- Share a specific **industry insight**.
- **Teach a skill** that genuinely helps the reader.
- Open up about a **professional failure** and what it taught you.
- Give frequent, specific **shout-outs** to people on the team.
- Make the **company's values** visible through real examples.
- Talk honestly about **what it is like to build** the company.

Active daily. And almost none of it is a pitch. It is all about adding value to the audience.

## Why the cadence pays off

The math is simple. When you show up consistently with useful thinking, you build an audience that is one to two orders of magnitude larger than the one you had when you posted quarterly. So on the rare occasion you actually need something — to hire, to fundraise, to find a partner — you are not shouting into an empty room. You have an audience that already trusts you and is willing to help.

The rules of engagement for executives have changed. Posting four times a year about your own milestones is not a presence; it is an occasional press release. A real presence is built in the weeks between the milestones, when you have nothing to sell and everything to teach.

The leaders who understand this early will own the attention — and the trust — long before they need to ask for anything.
